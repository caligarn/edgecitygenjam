FACTS & HISTORY

History rhymes.

Every big tech shift looks terrifying until the new creators show up.

  1. 1908

    Hollywood was born from rebellion.

    Filmmakers fled Thomas Edison's patent trust — the MPPC — and moved west to escape enforcement. That migration built the industry that defined the century.

  2. 1984

    The VCR was supposed to kill Hollywood.

    Sony v. Universal let home video live. Within a decade, video rentals earned studios more than the box office.

  3. 2007

    Netflix rewrote TV.

    Streaming turned the living-room set into a global on-demand catalog. Data picked hits. The world binged.

  4. 1999

    Napster couldn't kill music.

    It made streaming inevitable. Today artists earn more from streaming than CDs ever generated.

  5. 2010

    Instagram & Snapchat rewrote photography.

    Phones plus filters plus stories turned billions into daily photographers. Visual culture has never looked the same.

  6. 1975+

    Punk & hip-hop made cheap tools into genres.

    Four-tracks, turntables, photocopiers. Punk built a DIY label system; hip-hop sampled the whole record store. Remixing what already existed turned into two of the most influential movements in music.

WHAT DROVE THE GROWTH

Three inflections.

Each one unlocked a new wave of productivity — and a new reason to burn tokens.

  1. 01 2022

    ChatGPT.

    AI in your pocket, every day.

    The unlock: turned curiosity into habit. A billion people found their first reason to type at a model.

  2. 02 2024

    Reasoning.

    Models that think longer.

    The unlock: deeper work — code, research, analysis. Teams started paying for minutes of thinking, not milliseconds of reply.

  3. 03 2025→

    Agents.

    AI that takes action.

    The unlock: work happens while you sleep. Tokens now accrue in the background — a new economy of labor, denominated in compute.

    Reasoning.

    Walked in the front door.

  3. 03 THE CANVAS

    Agents.

    A studio that never sleeps.

    On set: overnight image and video generation — whole scenes rendered, iterated, and delivered before call time. The pipeline runs while the lot is dark.

MY MAIN ADVICE

How to prompt.

  1. TEXT

    Get the LLM to ask you questions — that’s how you reach deeper ideas and a better story.

    EXAMPLE“Ask me 5 questions to help me craft my story better.”

  2. IMAGES

    1) Nail the subject, objects & landscape. 2) Describe the camera type & angle.

    EXAMPLE“A lone red fox on a snowy hilltop at dusk, a tiny village glowing in the valley below — shot on 35mm film, low-angle wide.”

  3. VIDEO

    1) One action. 2) One camera motion. Keep it simple so the model stays coherent.

    EXAMPLE“The fox slowly turns its head toward camera — slow dolly-in.”

CORE PRINCIPLES

Ten rules that keep it alive.

  1. Every shot asks a question

    Keep them wondering what’s next.

  2. Conflict is oxygen

    No resistance, nothing’s happening.

  3. Every scene changes something

    Emotion, info, stakes, or status.

  4. One dominant emotion

    Make them feel one thing deeply.

  5. Raise the stakes

    Give a reason to care.

  6. Specificity beats generality

    Unique details are memorable.

  7. Start late

    Enter at the last possible moment.

  8. Show, don’t explain

    Let them connect the dots.

  9. Surprise every few seconds

    Keep introducing novelty.

  10. End early

    Leave right after it lands.

BEFORE YOU MAKE

Make it good.

A few habits that separate a good short film from a forgettable one.

  1. HOOK

    Grab them fast.

    Earn attention in the first 3 seconds — skip the slow intro and open on something striking.

  2. FOCUS

    One clear idea.

    A short film does one thing well. Cut every shot that isn’t serving it.

  3. SHOW

    Show, don’t tell.

    Let images and sound carry the story. Trust the audience to keep up.

  4. STAKES

    Something changes.

    A want, a problem, a turn. No stakes, no story — even in 60 seconds.

  5. SOUND

    Sound is half of it.

    Music and sound design do most of the emotional work. Never ship it silent.

  6. ENDING

    Land the last beat.

    The final shot is what people remember. Make it intentional.

WORKING WITH THE TOOLS

AI tips.

Get more from the tools with these habits.

  1. PROMPTS

    Be specific.

    Describe lighting, style, mood, camera angle, and subject in detail. With images, focus on subject, object, landscape, and camera angle. For video, specify one action and one camera movement.

  2. VOLUME

    Iterate fast.

    Generate many options quickly, then pick and refine the best one. Quantity unlocks quality.

  3. REFERENCE

    Use images.

    Upload a style or composition reference to anchor your results. Visual prompts beat words.

  4. MODELS

    Try different ones.

    Each model has its strengths. Switch if one isn’t working — the right tool for the shot.

  5. MOTION

    Keep it subtle.

    Short, simple camera moves work better than complex ones in image-to-video. Less is more.

  6. VIBES

    Embrace artifacts.

    AI weirdness can be a creative feature, not a bug. Lean into the strange — it’s part of the medium.

WORKING WITH THE TEAM

Human tips.

The tools are only as good as the team using them.

  1. ROLES

    Designate clearly.

    Assign Writer, Producer, Editor, and Animator before you start. Everyone knows their lane.

  2. STORY

    Agree first.

    Lock the concept and script before anyone touches the tools. Story leads, tools follow.

  3. PARALLEL

    Work side by side.

    While one person prompts images, another builds the edit timeline. Move on every front at once.

  4. SYNC

    Check in every 20.

    Short syncs every twenty minutes prevent the team from drifting in different directions.

  5. SHIP

    Done > perfect.

    A finished rough cut beats an unfinished masterpiece. Get to the end of the timeline first.

  6. TRUST

    Default to your team.

    If a teammate is closer to the work, trust their call. Speed beats consensus on a two-hour clock.
